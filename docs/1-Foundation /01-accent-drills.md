# Accent Reduction — Egyptian → Miami American
## 5 Sounds to Fix First. Everything Else Follows.

> **How to use this file:**
> Work on ONE sound per day. Do the minimal pairs first (10x each).
> Then do the work sentences (say each 3 times). Record yourself.
> Don't move to the next sound until this one feels natural.

---

## Why These 5?

These are the sounds that immediately signal "non-native" to an American ear.
Fix these five and 80% of your accent gap closes.

---

## SOUND 1: /p/ vs /b/

### The Problem
Arabic has no /p/ sound. Your brain maps /p/ → /b/.
So "plan" sounds like "blan", "process" sounds like "brocess", "API" sounds like "ABI".

### How to Make /p/
Put your lips together. Build air pressure behind them. Release it in a small **puff of air**.
Hold your hand in front of your mouth — you should feel the air hit your palm.
/b/ has no air puff. /p/ does.

### Minimal Pairs (say 10x each, alternate)
| /b/ | /p/ |
|-----|-----|
| ban | pan |
| bar | par |
| bill | pill |
| best | pest |
| back | pack |
| buy | pie |
| be | pea |
| base | pace |
| big | pig |
| bit | pit |

### Your Business Words with /p/ — Say Each 3x
- **plan** [PLAN] — "The PLAN is to automate the lead follow-up."
- **process** [PRAW-ses] — "Here's how the PROCESS works."
- **publish** [PUB-lish] — "We PUBLISH the content automatically."
- **platform** [PLAT-form] — "We connect your CRM to any PLATFORM."
- **proposal** [pruh-POH-zul] — "I'll send the PROPOSAL tonight."
- **pipeline** [PYP-lyne] — "Your sales PIPELINE will be fully automated."
- **API** [AY-pee-EYE] — "As long as it has an API, we can connect it."
- **package** [PAK-ij] — "My starter PACKAGE is five hundred a month."
- **project** [PRAW-jekt] — "The PROJECT is about 80% done."
- **pain point** [PAYN poynt] — "Let's talk about your biggest PAIN POINT."

### Full Drill Sentence
"The PLAN is to build a PIPELINE that automatically follows up with every lead, PUBLISHES the right content, and gives you a full PROCESS you can hand off to your team."

## SOUND 2: Short /æ/ — The "Trap" Vowel

### The Problem
Egyptian English tends to close this sound. "Can" becomes "ken", "have" sounds like "hev", "that" sounds like "thet".
American /æ/ is wide open — drop your jaw, spread your lips like you're smiling slightly.

### Mouth Position
Open your mouth. Say "ah". Now bring your tongue slightly forward and up. That's /æ/.
Feels awkward. That's correct. Keep going.

### Minimal Pairs (say 10x each)
| Egyptian-style | American /æ/ |
|----------------|-------------|
| ken | can [KÆN] |
| hev | have [HÆV] |
| thet | that [THÆT] |
| beck | back [BÆK] |
| mep | map [MÆP] |
| sed | said [SED] — (exception, stays /e/) |
| plenn | plan [PLÆN] |
| stend | stand [STÆND] |
| hend | hand [HÆND] |
| thenk | thank [THÆNK] |

### Your Business Words with /æ/ — Say Each 3x
- **can** [KÆN] — "We CAN connect your CRM to any platform."
- **have** [HÆV] — "Do you HAVE a CRM, or are you tracking in a spreadsheet?"
- **that** [THÆT] — "THAT's exactly the problem we're solving."
- **map** [MÆP] — "We MAP the data from your form to the database."
- **back** [BÆK] — "I'll get BACK to you by end of day."
- **plan** [PLÆN] — "Here's the PLAN for the next sprint."
- **track** [TRÆK] — "We TRACK every lead from first contact to close."
- **stack** [STÆK] — "Your tech STACK is perfect for this integration."
- **data** — Note: Americans say [DAY-tuh], not [DAH-tuh] or [DÆ-tuh]
- **that's** [THÆTS] — "THAT'S a hundred percent automation."

### Full Drill Sentence
"THAT's the PLAN — we MAP your data, TRACK every lead, and HAVE it all connected to your stack within forty-eight hours. You CAN be live by the BACK end of this week."

## SOUND 3: Word Stress on Business Terms

### The Problem
Egyptian learners often stress the wrong syllable. This makes words sound foreign even when the pronunciation of each letter is correct.

### The Rule
Most English nouns and adjectives: stress the **first** syllable.
Most English verbs (2+ syllables): stress the **second** syllable.

### Critical Business Words — Stress the CAPITALS

| Word | Wrong | Right | Example |
|------|-------|-------|---------|
| process | pro-CESS | PRO-cess | "Here's our PRO-cess." |
| system | sys-TEM | SYS-tem | "The SYS-tem handles it." |
| data | da-TA | DA-ta | "Your DA-ta is synced." |
| client | cli-ENT | CLI-ent | "Your CLI-ent gets a notification." |
| follow-up | fol-LOW-up | FOL-low-up | "The FOL-low-up is automated." |
| sequence | se-QUENCE | SE-quence | "The SE-quence fires in 24 hours." |
| platform | plat-FORM | PLAT-form | "Any PLAT-form with an API." |
| dashboard | dash-BOARD | DASH-board | "Log into the DASH-board." |
| database | da-TA-base | DA-ta-base | "Saved to the DA-ta-base." |
| integrate | in-TE-grate | IN-te-grate | "We IN-te-grate with your CRM." |
| automate | au-TO-mate | AU-to-mate | "We AU-to-mate the response." |
| record | (noun) re-CORD | RE-cord | "Pull the RE-cord for that lead." |
| record | (verb) re-CORD | re-CORD | "We re-CORD every interaction." |

### Drill — Say this paragraph, hitting every stress:
"Our SYS-tem AU-to-mates the FOL-low-up SE-quence. Every time a lead comes in, it IN-te-grates with your CRM, saves the DA-ta to your DA-ta-base, and sends a per-son-AL-ized re-SPONSE. You can see it all in the DASH-board."

## SOUND 4: Connected Speech — Gonna / Wanna / Lemme

### The Problem
Speaking word-by-word sounds robotic and foreign. Native speakers blend words together constantly.

### The Reductions

| Formal | Connected | Phonetic |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| going to | gonna | [GUH-nuh] |
| want to | wanna | [WAW-nuh] |
| let me | lemme | [LEM-ee] |
| got to / have to | gotta | [GAW-duh] |
| kind of | kinda | [KYN-duh] |
| sort of | sorta | [SOR-duh] |
| out of | outta | [OW-duh] |
| a lot of | a lotta | [uh LAW-duh] |
| what do you | whaddya | [WAH-dyuh] |
| did you | didja | [DID-juh] |
| could have | coulda | [KUD-uh] |
| would have | woulda | [WUD-uh] |

### Business Sentence Drills

1. "I'm GUH-nuh walk you through the entire SYS-tem real quick."
   `[Im GUH-nuh WAWK yoo throo thuh IN-tire SIS-tem REEL KWIK]`

2. "LEMme show you what this looks like from the client side."
   `[LEM-ee SHOH yoo wut this LUKS lyk]`

3. "I WAW-nuh make sure we're aligned before I start building."
   `[I WAW-nuh mayk shur weer uh-LYND]`

4. "We GAW-duh test this before we go live — otherwise we're gonna have problems."

5. "It's KYN-duh like a virtual assistant, but fully automated."

6. "We coulda done this manually, but that woulda taken your team twenty hours a week."

7. "Whaddya currently using to track your leads?"

### Full Connected Speech Paragraph
"I'm GUH-nuh walk you through what we're building. LEMme start with the lead sequence — because that's where most of your revenue is being lost. We WAW-nuh catch every lead within two minutes of them filling out the form. Right now, you're KYN-duh doing this manually, which means you're OW-duh bandwidth by Thursday every week."

## SOUND 5: Sentence-Final Intonation — Falling Pitch

### The Problem
Arabic uses rising intonation on statements. This makes you sound like you're asking a question when you're making a statement — which signals uncertainty and lack of confidence.

### The Rule
**Statements end with falling pitch.** Your voice goes DOWN at the end.
Questions (yes/no) end with rising pitch.
Questions (what/who/when) end with falling pitch.

### Visual Guide
- Statement: "This system will save you time." → voice: HIGH ↘ LOW at "time"
- Yes/No Question: "Does this make sense?" → voice: LOW ↗ HIGH at "sense"
- Wh-Question: "What does your process look like?" → voice: HIGH ↘ LOW at "like"

### Drill — Say these with FALLING pitch at the end:

1. "This is exactly what you need." ↘
2. "The system is ready to go live." ↘
3. "I've done this before." ↘
4. "We can have it running in two weeks." ↘
5. "That's the automation handling it." ↘
6. "This will save you ten hours a week." ↘
7. "I'll send you the proposal tonight." ↘
8. "The open rate went up to seventy percent." ↘
9. "Your team won't have to touch it." ↘
10. "That's the move." ↘

> **Tip:** Record yourself. Play it back. If it sounds like a question, say it again — lower the last word every time until it lands with confidence.

### The Confidence Signal
A falling pitch at the end of a statement says: "I know what I'm talking about. This is a fact."
A rising pitch on a statement says: "I'm not sure about this. Am I right?"
Clients hire confidence. End every statement going down.
